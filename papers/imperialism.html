Abstract

This paper examines the radical transformation of United States imperial ambitions and domestic democratic norms from the turn of the 20th century to the present day. In the early 1900s, a vibrant Anti-Imperialist League challenged American territorial expansion abroad, reflecting broad societal debate about empire. By contrast, contemporary American power operates through economic hegemony, military presence in over 750 foreign bases, and corporate-state fusion legitimized by Supreme Court decisions like Citizens United v. FEC (2010).

This paper argues that the shift from territorial imperialism to corporate-financial imperialism has coincided with the degradation of democratic participation and free speech, effectively creating a legally sanctioned oligarchy where concentrated wealth translates directly into political power. Through historical analysis and institutional examination, this research demonstrates how figures like Mitch McConnell have facilitated this transition by dismantling campaign finance regulations, while the Anti-Imperialist League's moral and constitutional arguments against empire have been entirely marginalized from mainstream political discourse.

1. Introduction

In 1898, when the United States acquired the Philippines, Puerto Rico, and Guam following the Spanish-American War, a powerful domestic opposition emerged that would fundamentally challenge the very concept of American empire. The Anti-Imperialist League, comprising prominent intellectuals, business leaders, and politicians including Mark Twain, Andrew Carnegie, and former President Grover Cleveland, articulated a principled opposition to territorial expansion on constitutional, moral, and practical grounds.

Key Contrast: Fast-forward 126 years to 2024, and the United States maintains the world's most extensive global military presence, with approximately 750 military bases in over 80 countries. Yet unlike the vigorous domestic debate of 1898-1920, contemporary imperial practices proceed with minimal democratic deliberation or dissent in mainstream political discourse.

This paper argues that this transformation reflects not merely a change in imperial tactics (from territorial acquisition to economic hegemony), but a fundamental restructuring of American democracy itself. The Supreme Court's decision in Citizens United v. Federal Election Commission (2010), which struck down restrictions on corporate political spending as violations of the First Amendment, represents the legal culmination of a decades-long project to equate money with speech and corporations with persons.

2. The Anti-Imperialist League and the Debate Over Empire (1898-1920)

2.1 Formation and Composition

The Anti-Imperialist League was founded on June 15, 1898, in Boston, Massachusetts, in direct response to the Treaty of Paris. The League's membership was remarkably diverse:

  • Political leaders: Former President Grover Cleveland, former President Benjamin Harrison, Senator George Hoar (R-MA)
  • Industrialists: Andrew Carnegie (steel magnate), who donated substantial funds to anti-imperialist causes
  • Intellectuals and writers: Mark Twain, William James, William Dean Howells
  • Labor leaders: Samuel Gompers (American Federation of Labor)
  • Reformers and activists: Jane Addams, Carl Schurz

2.2 Core Arguments Against Empire

Constitutional Arguments

The League argued that the Constitution provided no authorization for the United States to govern colonial subjects without their consent. Senator George Hoar articulated this position:

"The question with which we have to deal is not what we may do for these peoples, but what we may do to them... We have no right to acquire territory merely for the purpose of governing its inhabitants."

Moral Arguments

Mark Twain's satirical essays savagely mocked the hypocrisy of imposing American rule on unwilling populations:

"We have gone to conquer, not to redeem... We have pacified some thousands of the islanders and buried them; destroyed their fields; burned their villages, and turned their widows and orphans out-of-doors."

Economic Arguments

Andrew Carnegie argued that empire was economically irrational. He famously offered to personally purchase the Philippines' independence for $20 million to prevent American colonial rule.

2.3 The Significance of Public Debate

What is most striking from a 2024 perspective is not whether the Anti-Imperialist League succeeded, but that such a debate occurred at all. This stands in stark contrast to the 21st century:

  • The Iraq War (2003) was launched on false pretenses with minimal Congressional debate
  • Drone warfare operates with no geographic limits and minimal public accountability
  • Military operations occur in Syria, Yemen, Somalia, Niger, and elsewhere without Congressional authorization
  • The national security state operates with vast surveillance powers and minimal oversight

3. From Territorial to Economic Imperialism: The Mid-20th Century Shift

3.1 The Post-WWII American Empire

Rather than pursuing territorial expansion in the classical European colonial model, American policymakers constructed a system of economic and military hegemony that was arguably more effective and less politically costly.

The Bretton Woods System

The 1944 Bretton Woods Conference established institutions (IMF, World Bank) that would structure the global economy around American interests and the U.S. dollar as reserve currency. This "dollarization" gave the United States extraordinary power to:

  • Finance deficits by printing currency that others must hold
  • Impose sanctions by denying access to dollar-denominated transactions
  • Extract seigniorage from the global economy

3.2 Covert Operations and Regime Change

Where direct military intervention was politically costly, the CIA orchestrated coups and interventions:

3.3 The Military-Industrial Complex

President Eisenhower's 1961 farewell address warning remains prescient:

"In the councils of government, we must guard against the acquisition of unwarranted influence, whether sought or unsought, by the military-industrial complex. The potential for the disastrous rise of misplaced power exists and will persist."

Yet despite this warning from a five-star general and two-term president, the military-industrial complex has only grown more powerful.

4. Citizens United and the Corporate Capture of Democracy

4.1 The Decision

Citizens United v. Federal Election Commission 558 U.S. 310 (2010) overturned decades of precedent restricting corporate political spending. In a 5-4 decision, the Court held:

  1. Corporate independent expenditures are protected speech: The First Amendment prohibits government from restricting independent political expenditures by corporations
  2. Corporate personhood: For purposes of political speech, corporations possess the same rights as natural persons
  3. Anti-corruption rationale is narrow: Only quid pro quo corruption justifies restrictions
Justice Kennedy's Claim: "The appearance of influence or access, furthermore, will not cause the electorate to lose faith in our democracy."

Reality: Public trust in government has plummeted in the Citizens United era to historic lows.

4.2 Immediate Consequences

Growth of Outside Spending Post-Citizens United

Dark Money Explosion

  • 2008 election (pre-Citizens United): $338 million in outside spending
  • 2012 election: $1.4 billion in outside spending
  • 2020 election: $3.1 billion in outside spending
  • "Dark money" increased from $5.2 million in 2006 to over $1 billion in 2020

4.3 From Democracy to Oligarchy

Political scientists Martin Gilens and Benjamin Page conducted a groundbreaking analysis of 1,779 policy issues. Their findings were stark:

"The preferences of the average American appear to have only a minuscule, near-zero, statistically non-significant impact upon public policy... Economic elites and organized groups representing business interests have substantial independent impacts on U.S. government policy, while average citizens and mass-based interest groups have little or no independent influence."

5. Mitch McConnell: Architect of the Post-Democratic Order

5.1 Leading the Fight Against Campaign Finance Reform

From his earliest years in the Senate, McConnell focused obsessively on campaign finance - not to reform it, but to dismantle all restrictions on political spending. His opposition became the defining project of his career.

5.2 Strategic Court Appointments

Blocking Merrick Garland (2016)

When Justice Scalia died in February 2016, McConnell refused to hold hearings for Obama's nominee, inventing a "principle" that Supreme Court vacancies shouldn't be filled in election years. This held the seat open for Trump to fill with Neil Gorsuch.

Rushing Amy Coney Barrett (2020)

When Justice Ginsburg died just 46 days before the election, McConnell abandoned his invented principle and rushed Barrett's confirmation in 27 days.

5.3 Blocking Reform Efforts

Even as polls consistently show 70-80% support for overturning Citizens United (including majorities of Republicans), McConnell has blocked all reform efforts:

  • DISCLOSE Act: Would require disclosure of major donors - never received a vote
  • Constitutional Amendment: Senate voted 54-42 in favor in 2014, short of 67 needed
McConnell's Legacy: The result is a system where wealth translates directly into political power (via unlimited spending), courts protect this arrangement as constitutionally required, reform is nearly impossible, and feedback loops continually strengthen oligarchic power.

6. The New American Empire: Military, Economic, and Corporate Dimensions

6.1 The Global Military Footprint

As of 2024, the United States maintains approximately:

  • 750 military bases in over 80 countries
  • 173,000 troops deployed in 159 countries
  • Aircraft carrier strike groups capable of projecting power anywhere within days
  • Global surveillance networks monitoring communications worldwide

The United States is the world's largest arms exporter, with $175 billion in sales in 2020. This creates incentives for perpetual war: defense contractors profit from conflict, lobby for policies that create more conflict, and employ former officials who shape policy to generate future contracts.

7. The Degradation of Free Speech and Democratic Norms

7.1 The Paradox

Citizens United purports to expand free speech by removing restrictions on corporate political spending. Yet this "expansion" coincides with systematic suppression of grassroots speech:

Surveillance of Activists

  • Occupy Wall Street: Extensive FBI and NYPD surveillance, infiltration
  • Black Lives Matter: FBI classified as "domestic terrorism"
  • Environmental activists: Designated "eco-terrorists"

Criminalization of Protest

  • Anti-BDS laws: 35 states require contractors to certify they won't boycott Israel
  • Pipeline protest laws: Felony charges for infrastructure protest
  • Riot enhancement: Expanding "riot" definitions to criminalize protest

7.2 The Tiered System

Citizens United establishes that spending money is speech. The logical implication: those with more money have more "speech."

8. Comparative Analysis: 1900 vs. 2024

Dimension 1900 2024
Military bases abroad Minimal ~750 bases in 80+ countries
Anti-imperial movement Robust (Anti-Imperialist League) Marginal
Corporate speech rights Not recognized Unlimited (Citizens United)
Wealth concentration Gilded Age inequality Exceeds Gilded Age

Synthesis: Comparing 1900 and 2024 reveals that formal democratic rights have expanded while practical democratic capacity has contracted. In 1900, despite limited formal rights, democratic movements could effectively challenge concentrated power. In 2024, despite expansive formal rights, such movements face structural obstacles that render them largely ineffective.

9. Conclusion

This paper has traced the transformation of American power from the contested territorial expansion of 1898-1920 to the unchallenged global hegemony of 2024. The Anti-Imperialist League's vigorous opposition to the Philippine-American War represented a democratic polity grappling with foundational questions about empire and self-determination.

The contemporary American empire operates through economic hegemony, military basing, dollar dominance, and corporate integration rather than direct territorial control. This makes it simultaneously more extensive and less visible than classical imperialism.

9.1 Theoretical Implications

  • Classical theories of imperialism emphasizing economic drivers remain relevant but require updating for financialization
  • Liberal democratic theory's assumption that democratic institutions constrain concentrated power has been falsified
  • The concept of "free speech" requires critical examination when equated with spending
  • The relationship between domestic and foreign policy is tighter than often recognized

9.2 The Path Forward

Such a movement would need to:

  1. Build class consciousness about oligarchy and its mechanisms
  2. Connect domestic and foreign policy
  3. Create alternative institutions that prefigure democratic governance
  4. Challenge corporate power through labor organizing, antitrust, public ownership
  5. Demand democratic foreign policy through anti-war organizing
  6. Reform democratic institutions through voting rights, campaign finance, proportional representation
  7. Build international solidarity with movements globally
History is not predetermined. The trajectory from the Anti-Imperialist League's vigorous democracy to Citizens United's oligarchy was not inevitable; it resulted from specific political choices, judicial decisions, and movement defeats. Different choices, decisions, and movement victories could produce different outcomes.
