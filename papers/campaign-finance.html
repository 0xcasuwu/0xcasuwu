Executive Summary

This report provides a comprehensive analysis of campaign financing across the United States Congress for the 2023-2024 election cycle, documenting the flow of money from corporate interests, industries, and wealthy donors to federal candidates.

Key Findings:
  • Total Congressional Fundraising: $3.8 billion raised by House and Senate candidates
  • Record-Breaking: Total 2024 election spending projected to exceed $17 billion
  • Dark Money: $1.9 billion from anonymous sources
  • Lobbying: Record $4.4 billion in 2024
  • Concentration: Top 100 donors gave more than all 4.6 million small donors combined

1. Introduction and Methodology

1.1 Data Sources

Primary Sources:

  1. Federal Election Commission (FEC): Official filings from candidate committees, PACs, and party committees. Data downloaded February 6, 2025.
  2. Center for Responsive Politics (OpenSecrets.org): Aggregated and categorized data matching FEC filings to industry sectors.
  3. Individual Candidate Disclosure Reports: Quarterly and pre-election reports.

1.2 Classification System

Contribution Types:

  • Direct contributions: From individuals to candidates (limited to $3,300 per election)
  • PAC contributions: From registered Political Action Committees
  • Party contributions: From Democratic/Republican party committees
  • Independent expenditures: Spending "independently" of campaigns (unlimited)
  • Dark money: Contributions through 501(c)(4) organizations that don't disclose donors

12. Conclusions and Democratic Implications

12.1 Summary of Findings

Scale:
  • $3.8 billion raised by congressional candidates
  • $17+ billion total federal election spending
  • $4.4 billion in lobbying
  • $1.9 billion in dark money
Concentration:
  • Top 100 donors > all 4.6 million small donors
  • Top 0.01% = 40% of contributions
  • Industry PACs systematically fund oversight committees
Outcomes:
  • Policy follows donor preferences 70% of time
  • Members spend majority of time fundraising
  • Access proportional to donations

12.2 The Death of Representative Democracy

The data demonstrates that the United States Congress does not function as a representative democracy in any meaningful sense:

  • Representatives respond to donors, not voters: Gilens & Page (2014) found average citizens have "near-zero" policy influence
  • Donor access is 12x that of non-donors
  • Every major industry funds its oversight committees

12.3 Necessary Reforms

  1. Constitutional Amendment: Overturn Citizens United, establish that corporations are not persons and money is not speech
  2. Public Financing: Federal funding for campaigns, small-dollar matching
  3. Lobbying Reform: Multi-year cooling-off period, ban on lobbyist contributions
  4. Corporate Power Reduction: Antitrust enforcement, progressive corporate taxation
  5. Wealth Redistribution: Wealth tax, progressive income tax
  6. Labor Power Restoration: Card check union recognition, sector-wide bargaining
  7. Transparency: Real-time disclosure, end dark money
Democracy dies in darkness. This report brings light.
